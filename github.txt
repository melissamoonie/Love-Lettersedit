    <p>Dear GitHub, <br><br>

Out of all of the Git repository hosting services, you’re by far my favorite. Thanks for making me feel like people want to see my amateur coding projects even if that’s not true at all. Thanks for making me feel valued and thanks for doing such a good job of keeping track of when you and I hang out. I’m sorry that I haven’t seen you since May. All of those empty contribution blocks for June, July and August break my heart. I promise to change. I promise to be better. Maybe one day, I’ll even have some followers. I want our love to be public, especially since private love costs $7 per month and that’s not my style. GitHub, I adore the fact that you give out your love for free. I love using you for code collaboration and I appreciate you allowing me to borrow knowledge from those who are more talented than I am. I’ve always loved spooning but with you I can <b>fork</b>. Who doesn’t love forking and creating new, unique projects from existing ones without affecting the original project? I love using you to <b>push</b> my projects out into the word. With you, I feel like I can truly commit (<b>git commit –m </b> that is), and I’ll never forget my <b>first commit</b> to you. How romantic was that? By the way, your Octocat logo is pretty adorable. You’re like a social media platform for coding fanatics and that’s pretty cool. Goodbye Tinder, hello GitHub!</p><br>

    1) On your computer, move the file you'd like to upload to GitHub into the local directory that was created when you cloned the repository. <br>

    2) Open Terminal.<br>

    3) Change the current working directory to your local repository.<br>

    <b> $ git add . </b> <br>
    Adds the file to your local repository.<br>

   <b> $ git commit -m "Add existing file" </b> <br>
    Commits the changes.<br>

    Push the changes in your local repository to GitHub.<br>

    <b>$ git push origin your-branch </b><br>
    (Pushes the changes in your local repository to the remote repository.)<br>
